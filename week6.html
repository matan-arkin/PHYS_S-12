3D Design & Printing

Door Knob Cover - Background

The door to my room has a metalic round knob.





When someone opens the door, nothing stands in its way - causing an agressive bang. If left untouched, it proceeds to swing back near its closed position. Very annoying. Over the years, my wall has started to look like this:



Knob Catcher - Version 1

As a solution, I propose a 3D-printed "knob catcher." I was inspired by the discussion in class about flexible filament. After some research, I found the eSUN Elastic, which I would use for this design if I could actually print it (because this isn't essential for my final project and I live outside the US, probably we won't actually print this).

I started by choosing and measuring (with my caliper), all the parameters I was going to need. I measured the diameter of the knob (and divided by two to get the radius) - at its narrowest and widest points; I measured the length of the knob and I chose 4mm as the knob catcher thickness:



My design plan was: create two sketches on parallel planes with a distance between them equal to (10% more than) the length of the knob. Each sketch contained a pair of circles that create a ring with thickness equal to my defined "nob_cover_thickness." I would then "loft" the two rings. Unfortunately, after a fair bit of trying, I found that the loft feature could't use only the surface of the ring (the area in between the circles), but had to use the entire circle. I naively decided to extrude a cut down that meets the lower plane with the narrower ring. At first, it looked great!



Alas, when I exported to PrusaSlicer and tried slicing, I ran into trouble:



I couldn't understand why I was getting this mysterious "gap" between my cover's base and loft. I tried tweaking the SDL export settings, I tried installing Meshmaker so that I could export directly - nothing worked. I went back to Fusion 360 and examined the point of connection where I had seen the gap earlier, and zoomed way in. Seems perfectly normal!



As chance would have it, while I was wandering around my Fusion 360 sketch a while later, I happened to highlight this section:



It hit me. By giving up on the loft feature too soon and going with a regular cut, the cut was made straight down. With the difference in ring diameter between the upper ring and the lower ring, this probably caused the thickness of the cover to decrease as the cut went down, eventually reaching 0 and creating the gap we saw.

Part 2 - 3D Scan

It was the only free app that allowed me to scan 3D images without a LIDAR sensor. It did, however, charge $20 to export to STL, so I stuck with screenshots. This proved not a huge loss as the scans came out quite bad :)
