Week 12
Molding and Casting

Background

Ever wanted a cold drink in a bottle, but didn't have time to cool it down in the fridge? Did you also find yourself wondering why ice cubes aren't made to fit into the narrow opening of most bottles? I know I have. My goal in this assignment was to create an ice maker for ice that fits in bottles. Let's get started.




Part I - Molding

The first step was to find an object that could form the basis for my mold. I chose a small, cylindrical container of chap stick. I verified it fit into a standard bottle. Then I measured its height within my mold container. Its height roughly aligned with the 5.5 label.



I therefore opted to fill the container up to approximately the 4.5 mark in order to leave some spare room for displacement of the chap stick itself, while still not covering the object entirely and thus avoiding the need to cut off excess silicone at the end of the molding stage. With safety goggles, a KN95 mask, a long sleeve shirt, the gloves from the kit and open windows, here is me mixing in "B" first, then "A", then placing the chap stick:





I left the mold to harden overnight, outdoors. I feared indoors would be too cold and also didn't want to expose the house to any more potentially toxic fumes than necessary. If you look carefully, you can see how the silicone expanded slightly overnight - now reaching the "5" mark.



Luckily, the expansion wasn't significant enough to completely cover the chap stick, so I was still able to skip removal of excess mold material. Removing the chap stick without damaging/cutting the silicone proved a difficult task. I had to use my pliers and even then had to apply some serious force. The chap stick came out fractured and in two pieces, but the mold remained intact. Good thing I used a cheap object as the basis for my mold.



Part II - Casting

With the mold prepared, all I had to do was add water. I made sure to add as much water as I could so that, especially after accounting for expansion of ice, I would have a solid edge to grip from.





Several hours later in the freezer gave me a nice chap-stick-shaped ice cube!
